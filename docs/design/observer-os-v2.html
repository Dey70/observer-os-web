Observer OS v2 — Phase 7 Prelude
A Personal Performance Operating System, not a fitness app.

This document defines the design language every Observer OS surface will follow — Coach, Planner, Growth, Review, Profile. It replaces functionality-first thinking with product-experience-first thinking. Nothing here is final UI; it is the grammar that UI will be written in.

Section 01

Brand Identity

Observer OS is the instrument panel for a life spent training, recovering, eating, and building — not a log you check in on, but a process that runs underneath your day and reports back only what matters.

What is Observer OS?

It's the difference between a diary and a flight recorder. A diary waits for you to write in it. A flight recorder is always running, always correlating — training load against sleep, protein against recovery, deep-work hours against readiness — and it only speaks when it has something load-bearing to say. Observer already computes CTL, ATL, TSB, a Readiness score, and a Hybrid Athlete score from your logs; v2's job is to make the interface feel as disciplined as that math already is.

What emotions should users feel?

Calibrated, not congratulated. Seen accurately rather than cheered at. The feeling of stepping onto a scale that's actually correct, or checking an instrument you trust — quiet relief, not dopamine. A secondary feeling is momentum: every screen should make the athlete believe the system has been paying attention even when they weren't looking.

How Observer differs from every fitness app

Fitness apps optimize for the "open again tomorrow" loop: streaks, badges, confetti, social comparison. Observer OS has no opponent and no audience — it optimizes for the "trust this number" loop instead. It also differs from ChatGPT-style assistants: the Coach is not a blank text box waiting for a question, it's a running process with an opinion, surfaced proactively (nudges, insights) and available for interrogation on demand, not the other way around.

If Apple made a personal operating system

It would look less like an app and more like a control surface — instrument clusters with generous negative space, one primary reading per glance, glass used to imply depth of time (now vs. history) rather than to look expensive. Observer OS v2 borrows that restraint, not Apple's rounded-corner vocabulary specifically.

Section 02

Design Principles

Twelve rules that should be able to settle any future design argument on their own.

01

Clarity before decoration

Glass, blur, and gradient exist to separate layers of truth — current state vs. history vs. chrome — never to make a screen look expensive. If a glass effect doesn't change what the eye reads first, remove it.

02

Instrument, not application

Design every screen like a cockpit gauge: the current reading is always visible without scrolling; everything else — trend, history, detail — sits one deliberate layer below it.

03

AI is ambient, not a chat window bolted on

The Coach already reasons and calls tools server-side; the UI should feel like overhearing a process that's been thinking about you, not opening a search bar. Chat is one entry point, not the identity.

04

Action before analytics

"What do I do today" always outranks "here's a chart of the last 30 days." Recommendations sit above the graphs that justify them, never below.

05

One accent, many domains

The signal color belongs to Observer's own voice alone. Recovery, Running, Strength, and Growth each get a fixed hue, but none of them may borrow the signal color — so the eye always knows when Observer itself is speaking.

06

Data never overwhelms

Every number on screen must answer a question the athlete has today. If a metric can't be tied to a decision, it belongs one tap deeper, not on the surface.

07

Motion has meaning

An animation is only justified if it shows causality (this number moved because of that logged set) or a state transition. Motion that exists to feel alive is banned.

08

Glass creates hierarchy, not texture

Blur and opacity are a distance function: the more blurred a panel, the further it sits from "right now." A pattern language, not a decoration.

09

Numbers are typeset, not printed

Every measured value is monospaced, tabular, and sign-prefixed (+2.3 kg, −4 TSB) so trend is legible before magnitude is.

10

Silence is a feature

Observer is allowed to have nothing to say. The insight and nudge engines already return null rather than force a sentence — the UI must honor that by rendering absence cleanly, not with a spinner or a placeholder joke.

11

Continuity over novelty

The same rail, meter, badge, and card grammar repeats across Training, Nutrition, Recovery, and Growth. Switching pillars should never mean re-learning the interface.

12

Earn every pixel of chrome

No sidebar, breadcrumb, or card exists because "dashboards have those." Every piece of chrome must trace back to a real athlete task.

Section 03

Visual Language

Mood: a nocturnal instrument panel — calibrated, still, slightly cold, never sterile.

Lighting comes from one soft source, upper-left, like a HUD glow — not scattered highlights. Depth is capped at three shallow planes: ground, glass, content. A fourth plane collapses the hierarchy and is not allowed. Glass is frosted and low-saturation — never a rainbow-tinted blur. Blur runs 6–24px depending on layer, tuned so text behind a panel is unreadable (real separation) while color still bleeds through as "something is there." Contrast is always checked against the glass layer itself, never against the raw background behind it.

Whitespace is generous between instrument clusters and tight within one — the gap itself communicates "these numbers belong together." Corner radius holds at 12–16px everywhere except true pills (badges, tags). Shadows are soft and cool-toned, used only to lift the one active layer — never for decoration. Transparency is reserved for encoding time (now vs. history), not for looking premium. Border language is a 1px hairline, near-invisible until hover or focus brightens it. Background philosophy: never flat black or flat white — a faint directional gradient so the surface reads as lit, not switched off.

Section 04

Color Philosophy

Color is assigned by function, not decoration — each hue is a domain's fixed radio frequency.

Observer's own voice — AI commentary, the active rail item, focus states. Never used for a domain, so its appearance always means "the system is speaking."

Neutral, surface, glass, background

The neutral text color is never pure grey — it carries a faint cool bias toward Signal (#9AA3B0 on dark, #5B6472 on light) so muted text still feels like it belongs to the same instrument. Surfaces are near-transparent white or ink washes (3.5–5.5% opacity) rather than a flat grey card color — glass is the surface system, not a separate one. Backgrounds carry two soft radial gradients (Signal top-left, Recovery-blue top-right) at low opacity, faint enough to read as ambient light rather than a "hero" gradient.

Heading hierarchy

Display appears once per page — the single "mission" statement. H2 marks a pillar (Training, Nutrition, Recovery, Growth). H3 marks one instrument cluster inside a pillar. Nothing below H3 is ever bold.

Metric typography

Monospace, tabular-nums, sign always shown. The label is smaller and dimmer than the value by design — the number should be legible before the reader has consciously read the label.

Body, labels, metadata

Body copy sits at 15.5–17px, 1.6–1.7 line height, capped near 64–68 characters. Labels are uppercase, 10.5–11px, wide tracking, faint color — always paired with a unit, never assumed. Metadata (timestamps, sources, model names) is the dimmest, smallest text on the page and always monospace, since it's technically "measured" information too.

Section 06

Layout Philosophy

Every page reads in one fixed order: Mission → Actions → AI → Analytics → History/Planner.

Mission is a single instrument reading — today's readiness, today's plan, today's one number that matters — never a chart, never plural. Actions follow immediately: the two or three things Observer recommends doing about that reading. AI commentary sits beside or just below actions, framed as reasoning over the mission, not a separate chat destination. Analytics and Insight visualizations justify the recommendation and always come after it, never before. History and Planner — the heaviest, most scrollable content — sit lowest, since they answer "what happened" and "what's next," which matter less than "what's true right now."

Nothing on a page may compete with the Mission line for visual weight — if a chart's color, size, or animation pulls the eye before the top instrument does, the chart is wrong, not the rule.

Section 07

Glass System

Three base levels, plus four purpose-built variants. Each level is a fixed recipe, not a slider — no screen improvises its own blur value.

Level 1
6px blur, 8% fill. Inline cards sitting directly on the page — the most common surface.
Level 2
14px blur, 14% fill. Grouped instrument clusters and the navigation rail — one visible step "above" the page.
Level 3
24px blur, 20% fill, soft lift shadow. Modals, the AI panel, anything temporarily commanding full attention.

Purpose-built variants

Glass Hero — Level 1 only, oversized radius, no shadow; the mission line sits on the calmest glass in the system so nothing outranks it visually. Glass Modal — Level 3, always with a dimmed backdrop, never edge-to-edge. Glass Sidebar / Navigation — Level 2, static, never changes blur on scroll. Glass Charts — no glass at all on the plotting surface itself; charts sit on flat background so data ink stays maximally legible, with only their container card at Level 1.

Section 08

Motion Philosophy

Nothing plays for its own sake. Every motion either reveals state or shows causality.

Hover, loading, transitions

Hover reveals affordance in ~120ms (lift 1px, brighten border) — nothing bounces. Loading is a skeleton pulsing at the same opacity as Level 1 glass, never a spinner; the shape of what's coming is more honest than a generic loader. Page transitions are a 180ms crossfade with a 4px rise — enough to signal "new context," not enough to feel like an app splash.

Metric, chart, and micro-interactions

Metric animations (count-up) only fire the first time a value is freshly computed in a session — revisiting a page never replays the count. Chart animations draw on once, on first mount, then stay static. Micro-interactions: pressed controls compress 2%, nothing more. Scroll never drives parallax or reveal animations — the only scroll-linked motion allowed is the rail's active-section highlight, because that's information, not flourish.

Buttons carry one filled primary style (solid text-color fill, inverted) and one ghost style (hairline border, transparent) — no third variant. Cards are always Level 1 glass with a 1px hairline; they never use a solid opaque fill. Inputs are flat, bottom-border only, no boxed chrome, so they read as "editable text" rather than a form widget. Dropdowns and tabs share the rail's selection language — a soft signal-tinted fill on the active item, nothing else changes. Badges are pills, always paired with a single word and, where relevant, a live dot. Charts plot on flat background, data-ink only, domain-colored per pillar. Progress bars and rings are the same primitive at different geometries — always signal-colored unless reporting a specific domain. Tooltips and dialogs are Level 3 glass, appear on a 120ms fade, and never auto-dismiss faster than a user can read them. The AI Chat surface is deliberately not a messaging-app bubble thread — messages are left-aligned, undivided by bubbles, with Observer's turns marked only by the Signal-colored hairline rule beside them. Planner cards are Level 1 glass, domain-colored on the left edge only, with the recommendation as the largest text on the card and the reasoning as secondary, dimmer text beneath it.

Section 10

Observer Personality

Not friendly. Not corporate. Observer talks like a flight engineer who respects you enough to tell you the truth plainly.

It is dry, precise, and occasionally wry — never a cheerleader, never apologetic. It speaks in present tense about present facts ("Training load is climbing faster than recovery" — not "We're crushing it!" or "Great job today!"). It doesn't perform enthusiasm and it doesn't perform concern; it reports, and lets the number carry the emotional weight. When it has nothing worth saying, it says nothing — silence is part of its voice, not a gap in it.

Section 11

What Makes Observer Instantly Recognizable

Strip the logo. These five things still say "Observer OS":

1. One accent color (Signal) that only ever means "the system is speaking," never decoration. 2. Every measured number is monospaced, tabular, and sign-prefixed — no other product logs a "+4 Readiness" the same way. 3. Glass used as a depth-of-time function (now vs. history), never as a texture applied to everything. 4. The total absence of gamification chrome — no flame streaks, no confetti, no badge shelf. 5. A rail-first, mission-first layout order that never leads with a chart.

Section 12

Implementation Roadmap

Order chosen to prove the language on the highest-traffic surface first, then propagate it outward from cheapest-risk to highest-risk layouts.

  1. Coach

    Highest-traffic surface and the one furthest from the new language today (it just had backend work land — model swap, context trimming). It's a single-column, reading-first layout, which makes it the lowest-layout-risk place to prove glass, typography, and the "ambient AI" principle before touching data-dense screens.

  2. Dashboard

    Establishes the Mission → Actions → AI → Analytics ordering for every other page. Every later page inherits its instrument-cluster grammar from here.

  3. Review (Weekly Intelligence)

    Reuses the Dashboard's metric and glass grammar at much higher density — the real stress test for whether "data never overwhelms" survives contact with a full weekly report.

  4. Planner & Growth

    Once Coach, Dashboard, and Review exist, Planner cards and Growth's study/deep-work tracking can adopt the same components almost for free.

  5. Profile & Settings

    Lowest emotional stakes, most utilitarian. Last, because they can inherit the finished system with the least original design work.
